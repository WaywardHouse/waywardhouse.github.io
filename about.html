About

Never quite one lane

Wayward House is where I think out loud — in public, at length, with sources — about geography, systems, mathematics, and the places where those things collide with energy policy, the modern economy, and how we actually understand the world we live in.

I’m Paul Hobson. I trained as a geographer at the University of Manchester. As an undergraduate I processed satellite imagery for the European Space Agency, CNES, and NASA — earth observation missions tracking land surface, vegetation, and atmospheric aerosols across the planet. I spent time measuring glaciers in Zermatt. I started a PhD. I did a lot of work on it. I got sidetracked by research grants and Linux, which is a sentence that explains more about who I am than most job titles would.

What followed was twenty-five years of applying a geographer’s instincts — follow the systems, follow the incentives, follow the terrain — across some of the more interesting corners of the regulated economy: higher education in the UK and Canada, aviation safety through a global pandemic, energy infrastructure across a continent. Calgary is home now. Yorkshire is where I’m from. The basement is where the thinking happens.

What this place is for

Geography explains the past, illuminates the present and prepares us for the future. What could be more important than that? — Michael Palin

Palin’s right, and it’s frustrating that so few people act like it. Geography sits at the intersection of climate, economy, migration, and energy — and it is chronically underrepresented in the conversations that actually shape those things. Wayward House is a small attempt to remedy that, at least in the corner I can reach.

The work here is primarily analytical: essays that start from a falsifiable thesis, reason through the evidence, and arrive somewhere that might be uncomfortable. Some of it is computational — interactive models, live data, systems you can pull the levers on yourself. Some of it is pedagogical: I’m slowly rebuilding the environmental modelling course I once helped teach, for the tinkerers and explorers who never had the chance to take it. The mathematics is real. The geography is specific. The analysis is as honest as I can make it.

The Alberta material deserves a particular note. I live here, I pay attention, and I’m tired of the rhetoric — on all sides — that substitutes noise for geography. Alberta’s energy economy, its fiscal position, its relationship with the federation: these are tractable questions with real answers. I’m interested in those answers, not the performance.

The professional arc

The career doesn’t reduce to a single thread, which has occasionally been noted as a criticism and which I have always considered a compliment. The through-line, if there is one, is that I end up in situations where the organisation is at an inflection point, the technology strategy has not kept pace with reality, and someone needs to make sense of what’s actually broken before anything useful can be built.

Currently I lead technology platforms for Plains All American Pipeline — overseeing strategy across 18,000+ miles of pipeline and 140+ storage terminals in both the US and Canada. Before that: WestJet through pandemic collapse and operational recovery; the University of British Columbia, where I led enterprise architecture for nearly a decade and secured over $200M in capital investment; Cardiff University; and earlier still, direct research work on geospatial data with the European Space Agency and NASA.

I also hold an advisory committee role at Mount Royal University. I’m available for serious conversations about organisational transformation, technology strategy, and the kind of structural diagnosis that tends to get called for after everything else has been tried.

Why “wayward”

There is a chain pub called Wayward House. That’s not the reason, but it’s a good coincidence. I run a home lab and a study in my basement — SubHaus, which is exactly what it sounds like — and Wayward is what happens when you give a geographer a server rack and twenty-five years of accumulated opinions about how the world actually works.

The name is accurate in a few directions at once. I started a PhD and did not finish it. I volunteered as a glaciologist. I spent years processing satellite imagery for space agencies before anyone called it remote sensing on a CV. I led enterprise architecture for one of Canada’s largest universities. I am currently responsible for technology infrastructure across a major North American energy pipeline. I have been, at various points, a trail builder, a Scout leader, a geographer, an operations consultant, and a blogger. None of these things contradict each other, once you see the underlying system.

Wayward is a little cheeky. That felt right.
